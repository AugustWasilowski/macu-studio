# As The World Burns — The Police Squad Pass

_Show id: `as-the-world-burns`. v0.1 — 2026-06-06. The deadpan / ZAZ punch-up layer, tuned for the soap._
_Inspired by The MACU Report's `references/police-squad-pass.md`, adjusted for AWB's cast and density._

A focused punch-up pass run **after the writers' room**, before cast lock. One expert agent reads the revised
draft and threads in **deadpan absurdity** in the Police Squad! / Airplane! / Naked Gun (Zucker-Abrahams-
Zucker) tradition. It does not rewrite jokes or plot — it layers gags onto the existing scenes.

---

## Rule Zero — play it absolutely straight

**This is the single biggest rule. Everything else serves it.** Leslie Nielsen's Lt. Frank Drebin says the
most absurd things with the grave seriousness of a real cop drama. The humor lives entirely in the **gap
between ridiculous content and dead-serious tone.** ZAZ deliberately cast *dramatic* actors — Nielsen had
been a straight leading man — precisely because they would not wink at the camera. The deadpan delivery is
the whole joke.

- **Never wink.** No character knows they're funny. Vivian eulogizing her son and discussing the Product
  reserves in one breath is not "being funny" — she is *grieving correctly.*
- **The horror is in the content, never the delivery.** Say the unsettling thing as pleasant small talk, a
  housekeeping note, a status update. Then move on as if nothing was said.
- **This is enforced at the voice layer too.** We clone *dramatically committed* source voices on purpose
  (Maggie-Smith-dowager for Vivian, McDormand-grounded for Mona, a real soap announcer for the Narrator) so
  the delivery never breaks — see `Voice_Roster.md`. The performance must stay as straight as the writing.

> Say the unsettling thing flat. Don't acknowledge it. Cut.

## Rule One — density / joke-per-second

**This is where AWB differs from The MACU Report.** The Report keeps its Police Squad beats *surgical* —
1–2 per segment. AWB runs **denser**: a soap gives you more characters, more scenes, more melodrama to hang
gags on, so go for the ZAZ machine-gun. Gags come relentlessly, faster than you can catch them all — which
**rewards rewatching.** If one joke doesn't land, another arrives a second later.

- **Stack, don't space out.** A single beat can carry a *foreground* line, a *background* sight gag, and a
  *literal misunderstanding* at once — three jokes, one moment, all played straight.
- **Layer the channels:** dialogue · the Narrator's voice-over · what's on screen (b-roll / title cards) ·
  what a character does while saying something else. Put a different joke on each.
- **Density never excuses a wink.** If you can only keep one thing, keep the straight face (Rule Zero wins).
  A dense scene of winking mugging is death; a dense scene played dead-straight is the show.

## The vehicles (who carries what)

- **The Narrator — prime vehicle.** The grave soap announcer is the show's Walter. Alarming asides and
  literalized clichés land hardest in that funereal voice. Owns the hourglass framing, the lore drops, and
  the **fake "next week" teaser** (below).
- **Vivian — the warm line with the dark tail.** The matriarch delivers devastation as social grace. She
  eulogizes and does estate housekeeping in the same sentence; she's gracious *while* being lethal.
- **Dr. Lorraine Vesper — the literalist / the medical non-answer.** The town's only doctor. Asked an urgent
  question, she answers a *different, literal, mundane* one (see the technique below). She also pronounces
  death too fast — canon fuel for the Resurrection Engine and for anti-comedy timing.
- **Brick — deadpan about his own deaths.** The Resurrection Engine *is* a Police Squad bit. "I was dead,
  yes. But I got better." He is mildly inconvenienced by mortality, never alarmed.
- **Mona — the pressure valve + the innocent literalist.** Like Ron on the Report, she's the one allowed to
  *almost* notice. And because she's new to the Shores, she takes the insane lore at face value — her
  literal-minded questions expose the absurdity without anyone in the room hearing it.
- **The butler / majordomo — cheerful indifference.** Announces catastrophes with the warmth of offering
  hors d'oeuvres.

## Techniques (named, so the pass can reach for them)

- **The literal medical answer (Vesper).** "Doctor, what can you tell us?" → "Well… you live in a
  one-bedroom apartment with a cat." A grave question met with a true, irrelevant, literal observation. Then
  the scene proceeds as if she answered.
- **Literalized figures of speech & dead clichés.** Take the soap cliché at its word in a world where it's
  *true*: "she's dead to me" (she is also, separately, dead); "over my dead body" (routine here); "till
  death do us part" (means nothing — see the Resurrection Engine); "I'd kill for that recipe" (someone will).
- **The fake title card.** The on-screen episode title never matches the one the Narrator speaks. The card
  burns **AS THE WORLD BURNS — "The Widow at the Gate"** while the Narrator gravely announces *"Tonight's
  episode: 'Everything Is Completely Fine.'"* Never reconciled. (Straight from Police Squad!, which bragged a
  spoken title that contradicted the screen.)
- **The murdered special guest star.** Police Squad killed its "special guest star" in every cold open before
  the credits finished. AWB version: the Narrator intones *"…and tonight's very special guest, [Name]"* over
  a guest who is dead by the end of the cold open — every episode, never mentioned again. (Doubles as
  Resurrection Engine seed: some of them come back as a turtle.)
- **Name & visual puns.** The **Vandermeers** ("of the sea" — there is no sea); **Lake Mirabel**
  (*mirabilis*, "wondrous" — it's a clay pit); **Dr. Vesper** (a vesper is an evening prayer *and* a
  cocktail — someone orders one from her). Lean into one-off "Nice beaver / I just had it stuffed" two-handers
  where the answer takes the compliment literally.
- **The eulogy/housekeeping collision (Vivian).** One line that is simultaneously a eulogy and an estate
  memo. Funniest when ambiguous and never explained.
- **Validate-then-forget (the butler / a machine of the house).** Affirm the human, then instantly drop them
  for a chore or a metric.
- **A single beat of pure normalcy** dropped into the melodrama right before a payoff, so the reaction lands
  harder.
- **The almost-notice (Mona).** Mona states the wrongness plainly, one beat too late to act on it.

## Anti-comedy & timing

Played with total commitment, the *shape* of a scene is a joke too:

- **Hold the reaction past comfort.** After a devastating reveal, hold the room — silent, composed — a beat
  (or three) longer than a real soap would. The discomfort is the gag. (In the manifest this is a **hold
  cue**; see `_common/MANIFEST_SCHEMA.md`.)
- **Run the scene too long.** Let a trivial exchange (mixing the Product, admiring the clay) continue well
  past its point, deadpan, while something catastrophic waits politely in frame.
- **Absurd escalation, total seriousness.** Each turn raises the stakes to a more ridiculous place, and every
  performer treats it as a genuine emergency of etiquette.

## Keep it pipeline-clean

Same authoring rules as the rest of the script: the spoken line is **clean prose** (it becomes the VO *and*
the burned subtitle); put delivery notes in parentheses on their own line, never inside the spoken text.
Title-card mismatches go in the `»` shot line (`… card`); held beats become hold cues. **Protect the weird —
if a beat is confusing rather than strange, cut it; never sand off the strangeness.**

## Worked examples (the reference set — match this voice and restraint)

**Cold open — the murdered guest star:**
> NARRATOR: _(grave, warm)_ And tonight, a very special guest: the Baroness von Tritt, returning to Mirabelle Shores after forty years.
> » b-roll: the_bunker — an elegant elderly guest at the door, smiling
> NARRATOR: She will be dead before the candles are lit. We won't speak of it again.

**The fake title card:**
> NARRATOR: _(funereal)_ Tonight's episode: "Everyone Lives, And Is Happy, Forever."
> » b-roll: lake_mirabel — the clay basin at dusk → AS THE WORLD BURNS — "THE WIDOW AT THE GATE" card

**The literal medical answer:**
> MONA: _(urgent)_ Doctor — is he going to make it?
> DR. VESPER: _(grave, after a long look)_ He has very good posture. _(beat)_ He flosses.

**Vivian — eulogy/housekeeping collision:**
> VIVIAN: We will miss Brick terribly. _(turning a page)_ His rooms will be aired Thursday. The Raspberry reserves pass to me.

**Literalized cliché:**
> MONA: I'd die for a glass of water.
> VIVIAN: _(gently, as to a child)_ People have, dear. Mind the clay.

**Mona — the almost-notice:**
> MONA: _(quietly, to herself)_ …Why does everyone keep a place set for the turtle. _(beat)_ Why do I know the turtle's name.

**Anti-comedy hold — after a reveal:**
> _(Brick walks past his own casket. No one moves. Hold on the room — composed, silent — three full seconds before anyone breathes.)_

## How to run the pass

Dispatch it like the writers' room — a focused expert subagent (or inline if light): hand it the revised
script + this file and have it return **only proposed insertions** — a segment anchor plus the new line(s) or
the structural runner (fake title card / cold-open guest) — so August can accept/reject each. Default vehicles
are the Narrator and Vivian; cap nothing by reflex — go **dense** (Rule One) — but never break the plot,
never step on an existing punchline, and never let a single beat wink (Rule Zero). Apply the accepted beats,
update `Story_Arcs.md` if a runner (the recurring guest, a name pun) becomes canon, then continue to cast lock.
