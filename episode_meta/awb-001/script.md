**NARRATOR:** _(warm, hosting)_ And tonight, a very special guest: renowned water sommelier and accord-of-'09 signatory, Roland Fitch. _(a beat, unchanged register)_ Mr. Fitch will not be appearing this evening.
» b-roll: the_bunker — ROLAND FITCH in a good coat at the blast door, an open briefcase at his feet, the briefcase empty; he is already dead, and has been for some time

**VIVIAN:** _(presiding, immaculate, mid-eulogy, not a tremor)_ Brick Vandermeer was my son. He was thirty-one. He died, as the Vandermeer men always die, doing something he had been specifically told not to do. We will miss him. We have a great deal of him to miss. He has died before.
» Vivian core, in mourning black, standing at the head of an upright pale-wood casket in a candlelit bunker ballroom

**VIVIAN:** _(a small, gracious gesture toward the window)_ I asked that we hold the visitation here, by the lake. The clay is just gorgeous this time of year. You can see the strata. Brick loved the strata.
» Vivian core, gesturing to a porthole window framing the grey clay basin

**NARRATOR:** _(over the room)_ The Vandermeers own the most luxurious bunker on the shores of Lake Mirabel. They have owned it for three generations. They will tell you they summer at the lake. They are not lying. There is simply no lake. _(a beat, no inflection)_ There was, once — fourteen feet across, six inches deep. A plover lived on it. They called it the lake. The plover did not survive.

## ACT 1 — THE READING OF THE WILL

**VIVIAN:** _(to the assembled, the will held like a writ of state)_ The estate passes, as it must, to the eldest surviving Vandermeer. Which, as of Tuesday, is me. Again.
» Vivian core, seated at a long table, an envelope of heavy paper before her

**VIVIAN:** _(rising, ceremonial, lifting a tall glass pitcher from a velvet plinth)_ And with the estate passes… the Pitcher.
» Vivian core, both hands raising an ornate glass pitcher off a velvet display cushion, the room hushed

**VIVIAN:** _(the warmth of a corrected recipe note)_ Brick died over this. As did his father. As did his father's father. In each case, quite literally. We stopped saying "over my dead body" at family meetings after '87. It became logistically complicated.
» Vivian core, the Pitcher held aloft, perfectly serene

**NARRATOR:** _(reverent)_ The Pitcher. From the great barge that circled the dead world for forty years and ran aground in the founder's garden. They control the last drink powder on Earth. They call it, simply, the Product. Wars have been fought over the Raspberry Lemonade reserves. The accord of '09 is not spoken of.
» b-roll: the_product — a sealed foil sachet of pastel drink powder displayed under glass like a relic, soft votive light. BACKGROUND, soft focus: the butler at a side table tallies on a clipboard, writes a number, shakes his head, crosses it out, writes a smaller number, nods, satisfied, and leaves. Never addressed.

**VIVIAN:** _(measuring a single sachet, grave as a sacrament)_ One scoop. For Brick. We mix it, and we do not drink it. That is the custom. The waste is the point. That is what makes it a funeral.
» Vivian core, tipping a precise measure of pale powder into the raised pitcher, the act funereal

## ACT 2 — THE WIDOW AT THE GATE

**NARRATOR:** _(a turn, ritual, pure logistics)_ The mixing seals the door; by custom it does not open until the Product has settled, not one grain sooner. The Vandermeer blast door weighs four metric tons. Mona had knocked with her left hand, and switched to her right after the first hour. She ran out of things to think about after the second. By the third she had begun to feel she was owed a glass of water. She did not ask for one. She knew the customs by then.
» b-roll: the_bunker — the heavy blast door from inside, the great seal unlatching, a single fist striking it twice, dust falling

**MONA:** _(at the threshold, ash-grey, exhausted, certain — flat, counting)_ My name is Mona. I walked eleven days. My feet went dead somewhere on the second; you develop a system. I'm Brick's wife — his widow now, I suppose. I ran a grain depot before it burned. I know how a thing is counted, and how it's owed. I've come for what's mine.
» Mona core, a gaunt woman in a tattered coat standing in the open blast door, the dead clay flats stretching behind her

**VIVIAN:** _(a long, glacial appraisal, perfectly civil)_ My son had a wife. How wonderful. He never mentioned it — then again, he never mentioned a great deal. He was very handsome and almost entirely hollow. You'd have gotten on. _(a beat, turning back to the will)_ The estate is settled, dear. There is nothing here that is yours.
» Vivian core, turning to face the doorway, chin level, utterly unmoved

_(HOLD — ~4s, silent: Mona and Vivian regard each other. Far off, something structural in the bunker settles — almost an exhale. Neither acknowledges it. Vivian makes a small welcoming gesture: do take a moment. Mona does not take a moment.)_
» b-roll: the_bunker — the two women across the candlelit ballroom, motionless, the Pitcher glowing between them

**MONA:** _(not gawking — assessing the Pitcher the way she'd assess a stockroom, flat, professional)_ How much of it is left.
» Mona core, her eyes going straight to the glowing Pitcher on its plinth, reading it like inventory

**VIVIAN:** _(the first flicker of something — not fear, affront)_ We don't ask how much is left. _(recovering, gentle, as to a slow child)_ You really aren't from the Shores, are you.
» Vivian core, a thin patient smile gone briefly tight, then smooth again

## BUTTON — "I GOT BETTER"

**NARRATOR:** _(the hush before the sting)_ And it was then — at the reading of his own will, before his own casket — that the doors parted a second time.
» b-roll: the_bunker — the ballroom doors swinging inward, candle flames bending in the draft

**BRICK:** _(strolling in, alive, brushing clay from a beautiful funeral suit)_ Sorry I'm late. Roads. _(he glances at the clay through the window, approving, before anything else)_ …The strata's good this year. _(then, noticing the casket)_ Oh. Is that for me? That's thoughtful.
» Brick core, a devastatingly handsome young man in a dusty black suit pausing to admire the strata, his own open casket behind him, unbothered

**BRICK:** _(crouching to the casket, genuine professional appreciation, to no one)_ Is this walnut? I asked for walnut the last time. I got cedar. Nice gesture, but you can smell it thinking about rain. This has no cedar quality to it whatsoever. Good.
» Brick core, running a hand along the casket's grain, nodding, approving the woodwork at his own funeral

**MONA:** _(a whisper, the floor gone)_ …Brick. You were dead. They read the will. There was a viewing.
» Mona core, staring, bloodless, one hand to her mouth

**BRICK:** _(pleasant, final, already pouring himself a glass he absolutely should not)_ I was dead, yes. But I got better.
» Brick core, calmly lifting the sacred pitcher to a glass, the entire room frozen in horror behind him

**NARRATOR:** _(closing, grave, eternal)_ Next week, on As The World Burns: Brick drinks the funeral Product. A line is crossed. And somewhere, far out on the clay… something is walking in. _(a beat, flat)_ It is walking at roughly two miles per hour. It has been walking for some time. It has very good posture. That part is unusual.
» b-roll: lake_mirabel — the cracked clay basin at dusk, a single distant figure on the horizon → AS THE WORLD BURNS bumper
